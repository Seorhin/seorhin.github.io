I study how quantum states are structured in real physical systems — specifically in lanthanide atoms on surfaces, where atomic-scale symmetry, crystal-field effects, and subtle electron interactions shape magnetic properties that we can directly measure and model. My work sits at the intersection of synchrotron spectroscopy and multiplet calculations: I take experimental data and build theoretical models that account for physics the simpler models miss.

Research

Current · IBS Center for Quantum Nanoscience

Modeling the quantum level structure of surface-supported lanthanides

Lanthanide atoms on surfaces are promising candidates for atomic-scale qubits: their 4f electrons are well shielded, giving them long coherence times, and modern scanning probe techniques let us address individual atoms directly. But to use these systems deliberately, we need to understand precisely what quantum states they occupy — and that turns out to be harder than it looks.

My main project focuses on Er atoms on MgO. The conventional approach to modeling the crystal field — treating the surrounding atoms as simple point charges — worked for the oxygen top site but failed badly for the bridge site. At the bridge site, the predicted magnetic anisotropy was inverted compared to experiment, and ESR-STM showed a fast Rabi frequency where the model predicted driving should be difficult. Something important was missing from the picture.

My contribution was to extend the model by introducing partially occupied 5d configurations, allowing for hopping between the 4f-only ground configuration and states with ligand-to-5d charge transfer. This changes the effective crystal field in a way that simple electrostatic scaling cannot. I then used Bayesian optimization to fit the free parameters to the experimental g-tensor values from ESR-STM. The result: the new model correctly reproduces the magnetic anisotropy direction at the bridge site, and predicts which field geometry enables ESR driving — in agreement with experiment.

I am now extending this modeling approach to other lanthanide atoms: Ho, Tm, and Dy. Beyond the main Er project, I have been involved in synchrotron experiments. In one, focused on valence-electron magnetism in lanthanides on superconductors, I participated in the experiment and am now analyzing the XAS/XMCD data using IGOR Pro with sum rules — motivated in part by the possibility that 5d contributions play a role here too. In the other, aimed at probing element-specific spin polarization in CoTaS₂ (looking for evidence of triple-Q magnetic order), I joined the experiments at SOLEIL, France and the Pohang Light Source, Korea.

Earlier research

Scanning tunneling microscopy

Investigated YPc₂ molecular spin qubits on Cu(111) at IBS QNS. Learned surface preparation (sputtering, annealing, molecular deposition) and low-temperature UHV-STM/STS measurements. This experience introduced me to atomic-scale surface physics and motivated my current interest in surface-supported spin systems.

Trapped-ion quantum computing

Built a TTL pulse-controlled AOM and PID-based laser stabilization system at Ewha's trapped-ion lab. Diagnosed a signal distortion problem using Fourier analysis and corrected it by replacing a high-pass filter with Gaussian pulse convolution. The resulting system was operated via a GUI I wrote in MATLAB, and improved laser stability compared to the previous setup.

Research questions I am thinking about outside of my main project.

From states to paths: anyon exchange and the limits of synchronic analysis

Is analyzing the physical world through synchronic states — snapshots assigned to moments in time — a sufficient foundational strategy? The case of anyons puts pressure on that assumption. Two exchange processes can share identical initial and final states, yet differ in physical outcome because their trajectories in configuration space belong to different homotopy classes. The difference that matters is not captured by any synchronic state description; it lives in the global topological structure of worldlines across time. This suggests that trajectories, rather than states, may need to serve as the basic unit of analysis — and that taking this seriously also makes dimensionality a physically substantive parameter, since the homotopy structure of exchange paths is dimension-dependent. Upcoming presentation at the Korean Society for Philosophy of Science, July 2026.

Outreach & Teaching

Science communication · Board games

STM Monopoly

I designed an educational board game introducing scanning tunneling microscopy concepts and laboratory culture. It was featured at a Harvard–MIT open house and presented at the 2025 Korean Society for Science Education meeting. The game sparked the idea for Science Arcade — a game-based outreach program that QNS has since developed as part of its official outreach activities. I have continued contributing to the program, including through Schrödinger's Cat Party. Rulebook coming soon — will be available to download and play.
